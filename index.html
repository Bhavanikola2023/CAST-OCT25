What AI Can Do for Your Work

For Practitioners
CAC Staff
  • Draft training protocols and education materials
  • Generate fictional case scenarios for development
  • Create presentation templates
  • Synthesize research for evidence-based practice
For Faculty
Educators
  • Develop syllabi and course outlines
  • Design assessments and rubrics
  • Find relevant research and statistics
  • Create engagement activities
For Leaders
Administrators
  • Write grant applications and impact reports
  • Analyze program data for stakeholders
  • Develop strategic plans and policies
  • Create board presentations
⚠️ What AI Cannot Do: Replace trauma-informed clinical judgment, handle confidential case information, verify accuracy of legal requirements automatically, make ethical decisions about child welfare cases, or conduct actual forensic interviews.

Ethical Framework

🚫 RED LINES - Never Use AI For:

  • Real victim/client identifying information
  • Actual case documentation or forensic interview notes
  • Confidential MDT case discussions
  • Real court testimony preparation

✅ GREEN ZONES - Appropriate Use:

  • Fictional case scenarios for training
  • Template development
  • Literature reviews (with verification)
  • Administrative documents
🔑 Golden Rule: If it involves real children, real cases, or confidential information, AI stays out.

⚖️ Ethics & Best Practices Guide

▼

The RED LINES - What to NEVER Put into AI

❌ Absolutely Prohibited Content

  • Real victim/client identifying information (names, addresses, dates of birth)
  • Actual case details or forensic interview content
  • Confidential MDT case discussion notes
  • Real photographs or evidence from cases
  • FERPA-protected student records
  • HIPAA-protected health information
  • Sealed court records or grand jury materials

Why This Matters

Legal Obligations

FERPA, HIPAA, state confidentiality laws require protecting client information

Ethical Duty

Professional codes for social workers, psychologists, attorneys mandate confidentiality

Data Security

AI companies may use inputs for training; data may not be secure

Harm Prevention

Protecting vulnerable children and families from privacy violations

The GREEN ZONES - Appropriate AI Use

✅ What IS Safe and Appropriate

  • Fictional scenarios and training materials
  • Templates and frameworks (before adding real information)
  • Administrative and educational content
  • Research and literature synthesis
  • Generic professional content

The De-identification Test

Before using any case information, ask:

  1. Could this information identify a specific child or family?
  2. Could someone familiar with our community recognize who this is about?
  3. Am I comfortable with this information being stored on AI company servers?
  4. Have I removed ALL identifying details?

If you answered "yes" to #1 or #2, or "no" to #3, DO NOT input it into AI.

Verification Requirements - Always Check

Legal Information
  • State-specific mandated reporting laws
  • Court procedures and requirements
  • Evidence rules and statute of limitations
  • Jurisdictional variations
Statistical Data
  • Child maltreatment incidence rates
  • Demographics of abuse victims
  • Service utilization statistics
  • Research findings and outcome data
Clinical/Medical Info
  • Signs and symptoms of abuse
  • Developmental milestones
  • Treatment recommendations
  • Trauma effects and responses
Local Resources
  • Service availability and eligibility
  • Contact information for agencies
  • Program costs and requirements
  • Current organizational policies

Quality Assurance Checklist

Before using ANY AI-generated content:

  • All factual claims verified against authoritative sources
  • Statistics checked and properly cited
  • Legal information confirmed with current laws
  • Language is trauma-informed and empowering
  • Content is culturally competent and inclusive
  • No identifying information included
  • Original sources cited (not AI)

When NOT to Use AI

⚠️ Situations Where AI Is Inappropriate:

  • Clinical Decision-Making: Determining if a child is safe, assessing risk levels, making treatment recommendations
  • Legal Decisions: Determining probable cause, deciding on charges, assessing evidence admissibility
  • Sensitive Communications: Disclosing abuse, communicating case outcomes, therapeutic interventions
  • Unique Professional Judgment: Complex ethical dilemmas, unusual circumstances, high-liability situations

🔑 The "Human-in-the-Loop" Principle

Always ask: "Is AI helping me do my work better, or is it doing the work for me?"

If it's the latter, pause and reconsider.

📋 Quick Reference Card

▼

🎯 5 Questions Before Using AI

  1. Does this involve real people or confidential information?
    → If YES: Don't use AI
  2. Will I verify all factual claims?
    → If NO: Don't use AI or commit to verifying
  3. Have I reviewed for trauma-informed and culturally competent language?
    → If NO: Review before using
  4. Am I using AI to enhance my expertise or replace it?
    → If REPLACE: Reconsider
  5. Would I be comfortable if this AI input/output became public?
    → If NO: Don't input

When in doubt, leave it out.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Problem: Output isn't what I needed

Solution: Be more specific in your prompt. Add details about audience, length, tone, format. Include examples.

Problem: Content seems generic

Solution: Add context to your prompt. Specify your community, organization type, populations served, relevant constraints.

Problem: Don't know how to start

Solution: Use basic structure: "Create a [what] for [audience] about [topic]. Include: [elements]. Tone: [how it sounds]"

Problem: AI included incorrect info

Solution: ALWAYS verify. Check statistics, legal requirements, clinical info. AI provides drafts; YOU provide accuracy.
